Chapter 01 — The Problem
"If detected early, your chances of surviving breast cancer are over 99%. Wait too long — it drops to just 27%."

Breast cancer is the most common cancer among women worldwide. In India alone, cases have risen 50% over the past decade. A lump appears when breast tissue grows uncontrollably. These lumps are either benign (harmless, slow-growing) or malignant (dangerous, fast-spreading).

The gap between those two numbers is everything. And yet, the tools doctors use today — biopsies, ultrasounds, microscopes — all depend on human interpretation. And humans disagree.

⚠ The Disagreement Problem
Pathologists agree on biopsy readings only 75–96% of the time, depending on case difficulty. Ultrasounds are even harder to interpret when breast tissue is dense. This 25% variability is not acceptable when lives are at stake.

Manual diagnosis is also expensive, slow, and prone to missing subtle early signs. As medical imaging grows globally — especially in low-resource settings — the world needs a tool that is consistent, fast, and scalable.

💡 The Core Question
Can we train a computer to look at a breast scan or tissue sample and tell us — reliably — whether it's cancerous? And can it do this better, faster, and more consistently than a human?
Chapter 02 — The Solution

Transfer Learning:
Teaching old models new tricks

Deep learning models need enormous amounts of training data. Medical datasets are tiny. This creates a problem — but Transfer Learning solves it brilliantly.

🧠 What is Transfer Learning?
Imagine hiring a chef who already knows how to cook Italian food and then training them to cook Indian food. They don't start from zero — they already understand heat, seasoning, texture. Transfer learning does the same: take a model pre-trained on millions of general images (ImageNet), then fine-tune it on a specific medical dataset. The model already "knows" edges, shapes, and textures. You just teach it what cancer looks like.

This paper tests four pre-trained deep learning models on breast cancer images from two completely different sources:

Chapter 03 — How It Actually Works

The pipeline:
From raw scan to diagnosis

1
Data Collection
Download BUSI and BreakHis datasets from Kaggle. Organize by class (benign/malignant/normal) and magnification level. Store locally.
2
Preprocessing — Resize & Normalize
All images resized to 224×224 pixels (3 color channels). Pixel values normalized to [0,1]. This matches what each pretrained model expects from ImageNet training.
3
Data Augmentation (Training Set Only)
To prevent overfitting on small datasets: rotate ±20°, flip horizontally, shift width/height ±20%, zoom ±20%, shear 0.1. This artificially multiplies data diversity. Validation and test sets are NOT augmented — to get real-world performance numbers.
4
Transfer Learning — Freeze the Backbone
Load pretrained model weights (from ImageNet). Freeze all convolutional layers — they already know how to extract features. Only the classification "head" is trained on medical data. This is faster and prevents overfitting.
5
Custom Classification Head
Global Average Pooling → compresses feature maps to a vector
Dense (256 neurons, ReLU) → learns complex patterns
Dropout (0.55) → randomly disables 55% of neurons during training to prevent memorization
Softmax output → 3 classes for BUSI, 2 for BreakHis

Why 0.55 dropout specifically? At 0.5, the model overfit after 12 epochs. At 0.7, it couldn't learn at all (accuracy stuck below 75%). 0.55 was the sweet spot.
6
Optimizer Battle: Adam vs AdamW vs SGD
Tested three optimizers:
Adam — fastest convergence, best validation accuracy ✓
AdamW — adds weight decay, but no benefit here since dropout already handles regularization
SGD — slow, sensitive to learning rate, no improvement

Winner: Adam with learning rate = 0.0001. Trained for 30 epochs, batch size 32.
7
Early Stopping (Patience = 7)
If validation loss doesn't improve for 7 consecutive epochs, training stops automatically and best weights are restored. Prevents wasted computation and overfitting.
8
Evaluation Metrics
Accuracy — overall correct predictions
Precision — of predicted cancers, how many are actually cancer?
Recall — of actual cancers, how many did we find?
F1 Score — harmonic mean of precision and recall
AUC-ROC — model's ability to distinguish classes across thresholds
Confusion Matrix — visual breakdown of where the model got confused

🏆 Why EfficientNetB0 Won
EfficientNetB0 uses compound scaling — it scales depth, width, and resolution simultaneously rather than in isolation. This makes it exceptionally efficient per parameter.

It achieved highest accuracy with 4.8× fewer parameters than ResNet50 and 26× fewer than VGG16. In real-world healthcare, this means faster inference on cheaper hardware — critical for low-resource settings.
Confusion Matrix Insight

On the BUSI dataset (3 classes): The model correctly classified most benign cases (86/134) but struggled with normal tissue — 21 normal samples were misclassified as malignant. This is clinically acceptable — it's safer to over-flag than under-flag cancer.

On BreakHis (2 classes): Strong performance — 323 benign correctly classified, 766 malignant correctly classified. Only 49 in each class were misclassified — impressive on a 1,186-sample test set.

⚡ Future Directions
Limitations include: models work best on high-resolution, low-noise images. Future work will explore noise filtering, image enhancement, and Vision Transformers (ViTs) for even better performance. External validation on new hospital datasets is also needed before clinical deployment.

Chapter 07 — Glossary

Terms you must know

Transfer Learning
Using a model pre-trained on large data (ImageNet) and fine-tuning it for a new, smaller dataset. Leverages existing knowledge instead of training from scratch.
Benign vs Malignant
Benign tumors grow slowly and don't spread. Malignant tumors grow rapidly and invade nearby cells — these are cancerous and life-threatening.
Overfitting
When a model memorizes training data and fails on new data. Prevented here using dropout (0.55), data augmentation, and early stopping.
Dropout
Randomly disabling a fraction of neurons during training. Forces the network to learn redundant representations — a regularization technique.
Adam Optimizer
Adaptive Moment Estimation. Adjusts learning rate per parameter using first and second moment estimates. Fast convergence, works well on sparse gradients.
AUC-ROC
Area Under the Receiver Operating Characteristic curve. Measures how well the model distinguishes between classes across all decision thresholds. 1.0 = perfect.
Confusion Matrix
A grid showing True Positives, True Negatives, False Positives, and False Negatives. Visually reveals where the model makes mistakes.
Early Stopping
Automatically halt training when validation loss stops improving for N epochs (patience). Best weights are saved and restored — prevents wasteful over-training.
Global Average Pooling
Takes the average value of each feature map to produce a single number per map. Converts 3D feature volume to a flat 1D vector for classification.
Stratified Sampling
Splitting data while maintaining class proportions in each split. Prevents a situation where one class dominates the training or test set.
Histopathology
Study of diseased tissue under a microscope. BreakHis provides these high-res tissue slide images at 40x, 100x, 200x, 400x magnification.
EfficientNetB0
A model that scales network depth, width, and input resolution together (compound scaling) for maximum efficiency. 5.3M parameters, best accuracy here.
